This essay will critically examine the human-dog relationship, how it was developed, the factors sustaining the relationship, and the science, and history behind the human-canine bond.As thousands of years passed and humankind evolved and the world changed, the bond between man and dog has not wavered. Anders Bergstrom, a geneticist at the Francis Crick Institute, stated in an interview, "We find that when we compare the history of dogs to the history of humans, to a quite large degree they mirror each other" This bond has been sustained because of three main factors; domestication, mutualistic symbiosis, and dogs' emotional intelligence. The domestication of dogs is the process by which wolves evolved into our modern-day dog. Domesticated animals are animals that have been selectively bred and genetically adapted over generations to live alongside humans. Humans have selected specific traits of dogs to breed to make them a better fit for our society. For example, large and strong dogs such as Mastiffs started being used as guard dogs while small dogs with stronger noses such as beagles were used for hunting. As dogs provide humans with specific qualities humans provide dogs with food, shelter, and companionship. This is called mutualistic symbiosis. According to an article by Biology Online, mutualistic symbiosis is defined as, “a symbiotic relationship between individuals of different species in which both individuals benefit from the association. In this type of symbiosis, both organisms of different species rely on one another for nutrients, protection, and other life functions; hence, they are usually found living in close proximity”(). Both humans and dogs both benefit from their association and this has helped keep their bond strong. Finally, like humans, dogs are social creatures. Dogs can interpret and reciprocate human emotions, which helps humans see them as companions. In 2016, six scientists came together to test if dogs could understand human emotions. They presented dogs with either human or dog faces with different emotions such as happy, playful, angry, or aggressive. It is paired with a single vocalization from the same individual, either positive or negative. They found that the dogs looked significantly longer at the face whose expression matched the vocalization. This was an ability previously only seen in humans. These results demonstrate that dogs can understand and interrupt visual and vocal emotions, and can discriminate between positive and negative emotions from humans().
